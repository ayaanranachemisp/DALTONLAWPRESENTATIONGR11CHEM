Slide 1: Core Theory of Dalton’s Law

  • Scientific Definition: The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting ideal gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas.
  • Independence in Dalton’s Law: Each gas component behaves as if it completely occupies the container alone. It exerts the same pressure it would if it were the only gas present at that volume and temperature.
  • Water Vapor Factor: When collecting gas over water (a common lab research need), you must account for the water vapor that naturally mixes with the gas.

The Main Formula

PTotal = PN2 + PO2 + PAr + PCO2

NOTE: PDry Gas = PTotal - PWater Vapor

Slide 2: Historical Context and Atmospheric Research

  • The Scientist: John Dalton (1766–1844), an English chemist and physicist.
  • Dalton’s Past: John Dalton was a dedicated meteorologist who recorded daily weather logs for 57 years. His law didn’t come from some random thought, it was born from his Atmospheric Humidity Studies.
  • The Research Need: In the early 1800s, scientists were trying to understand how weather worked. Dalton needed to explain how water vapor existed in the air. He realized that the atmosphere isn't one "thing," but a mixture of separate parts.
  • The “Big Shift”: He moved descriptions of gas away from abstract "mysterious substances" and into strict, measurable particle counts and mathematical relationships.
  • Dalton’s Key Discovery: He proved that atmospheric pressure is not one gas pushing on us, but the combined independent weight of all gases in the mixture.

Dalton's Atmospheric Insight

Instead of viewing air as a single, uniform substance, Dalton realized it is actually a crowded room of independent gas particles, all bouncing around and exerting their own pressure at the same exact time.

Slide 3: KMT Connection

  • Molecular Motion Recap: Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) says gas particles are always moving randomly. Gas pressure is caused entirely by these particles crashing into the container walls.
  • Tons of Empty Space: Gas particles have negligible volume (they are so tiny compared to the huge empty space around them that they never crowd or block each other).
  • Zero Intermolecular Forces: Different gas types completely ignore each other. They have no attraction or repulsion, so O2 molecules bounce around like N2 molecules don't even exist.
  • These collisions just add up: Because they act completely alone, their collisions with the walls simply add up.
  • The Bottom Line: Dalton’s Law is just the real-world proof that gas particles act completely independent on the microscopic level.
More Total Moles (n) → More Wall Collisions → Higher Total Pressure (PTotal)

Slide 4: Mole Fraction and Calculations

This slide covers the mathematical theory and the procedural steps for solving problems.

  • Mole Fraction (Xi): A ratio showing what fraction of the total particles belongs to a specific gas.
  • Relation to Pressure: A gas’s partial pressure is directly proportional to its mole fraction.
  • The Three Necessary Steps to Solve:
    1. Aggregate: Sum all individual moles to find nTotal.
    2. Fraction: Divide the target gas moles by nTotal to get the decimal ratio (Xi).
    3. Multiply: Multiply that decimal by the absolute PTotal to find the partial pressure.
  • ALWAYS REMEMBER: Always convert all values to the same unit before calculating.

Mole Fraction Formula

Xi = ni / nTotal

Partial Pressure Formula

Pi = Xi × PTotal
The Golden Rule: You can never add pressures with different units! Use these first:
1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg

Slide 5: Real-World Applications and Diving Physics

  • Gas Biology: The human body responds to individual partial pressures (Pi), not the general gas percentages.
  • Scuba Diving and Hydrostatic Pressure: For every 10 meters a diver descends into the ocean, the water weight adds 1 whole atm of total pressure. As PTotal multiplies underwater, the partial pressure (Pi) of every gas in the diver's lungs spikes too (Pi = Xi × PTotal).
  • The Chemical Consequences:
    • Oxygen Toxicity: If PO2 goes above 1.4 atm (which happens at just 30m depth on regular air), oxygen becomes toxic to the nervous system and causes sudden seizures.
    • Nitrogen Narcosis: High PN2 forces gas into nerve membranes, causing a dangerous feeling of drunkenness and impaired judgment.
  • The Engineering Solution (Nitrox/Trimix): Divers mix custom gas blends. By lowering the mole fraction (Xi) of oxygen or nitrogen in the tank, they force the partial pressures (Pi) to stay at safe, non-lethal levels even under extreme underwater pressure.

Diving Physics Formula

Pi = Xi × PTotal

Altering Xi counteracts spikes in high hydrostatic pressure environmental systems.
