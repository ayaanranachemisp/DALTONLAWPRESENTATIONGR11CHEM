01 / Partial Pressure: Main Idea

  • Gas Mixtures: Real-world gases (like air) are almost always mixtures of multiple independent components.
  • Partial Pressure: The individual pressure exerted by a single gas type inside a mixed container.
  • Independent Space: Each gas component behaves as if it completely occupies the container alone.
  • Dalton's Law: The total pressure of a non-reactive gas mix is exactly equal to the sum of each gas's individual partial pressure.
  • Why it matters: Total pressure alone doesn't show biological safety or distinct chemical reactivity limits.

Primary Relationship

PTotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...

Example for Atmospheric Blends:
PTotal = PO2 + PN2 + PCO2

02 / John Dalton Background

  • John Dalton (1766-1844): English chemist, physicist, and prominent early meteorologist.
  • Atmospheric Humidity Studies: Explored how water vapor pressure directly alters standard dry atmospheric gas totals.
  • Core Discovery: Discovered that PTotal = PDry Air + PWater Vapor when water vapor evaporates into a sealed dry system.
  • Shift to Quantitative Chemistry: Moved gas descriptions away from abstract ideas into strict, absolute particle counts.

Key formula from this section

PTotal = PDry Air + PWater Vapor

This is the same idea as Dalton's Law: each gas adds its own pressure to the total.

03 / Kinetic Molecular Theory

  • Particle Motion: Gas particles move randomly at constant speeds, causing pressure by bouncing off walls.
  • Ideal Gas Interactions: Gas particles have negligible volume and zero intermolecular attractions.
  • Independent Collisions: Different gas types bounce off container walls without altering neighboring particles.
  • Additive Force: Total pressure is simply the cumulative impact of all independent molecular collisions.

Different gases hit the walls independently, so their pressures add together.

04 / Mole Fraction Practice

  • Mole Fraction (Xi): The ratio of target gas moles divided by total system moles (Xi = ni / nTotal).
  • Pressure Calculation: Individual partial pressures match mole ratios directly (Pi = Xi · PTotal).
  • Strict Units Rule: All pressure values must be converted to identical units before adding them together.
  • Constants: 1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg = 760 torr.
  • Worked Scale: 2 mol O₂ + 3 mol N₂ at 500 kPa total results in PO2 = (2/5) · 500 kPa = 2.00 × 10² kPa.

06 / Real World Uses

  • Atmospheric Makeup: Earth's air exerts roughly 101.3 kPa at sea level, combining independent contributions of N₂ (~79.0 kPa) and O₂ (~21.3 kPa).
  • Scuba Safety Limits: High total pressure underwater spikes gas partial pressures, risking oxygen toxicity or sudden nitrogen narcosis.
  • Pneumatic Gas Collection: Lab gases trapped over water mix naturally with rising water vapor.
  • Vapor Correction: Scientists subtract water vapor pressure from measured values to find true dry gas quantities (PDry Gas = PTotal - PWater Vapor).

Vapor Correction

PTotal = PDry Air + PVapor

Essential for pneumatic displacement calculations.

07 / Film Canister Demo Safety

Tap each step when your group is ready. This station is meant to be used before the film canister pop demo.

  • Goggles on: Everyone close to the demo wears safety goggles before the tablet is added.
  • Clear space: Keep faces, lights, phones, and breakable objects away from the canister.
  • Water first: Add a small amount of water to the film canister.
  • Tablet fast: Place the tablet in quickly, snap the lid on tightly, and set the canister lid-side down.
  • Step back: Do not lean over it, pick it up, or touch it while pressure is building.
  • Clean up: After it pops, wait a moment, then wipe the table and throw away tablet pieces.
Why it pops: The tablet makes CO₂ gas. Gas pressure builds inside the closed canister until the lid seal gives way.

Fast Order

  1. Goggles on
  2. Clear the table
  3. Water in canister
  4. Tablet in quickly
  5. Lid on tightly
  6. Place lid-side down
  7. Step back
